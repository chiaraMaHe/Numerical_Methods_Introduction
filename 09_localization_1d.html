Exercise 9 - Localization due to softening¶

Consider a bar of Length $L$ under uniaxial extension with the following bilinear constitutive behaviour:

$$ \sigma = \begin{cases} \displaystyle E \epsilon &\epsilon \leq \epsilon_\text{f} = \displaystyle \frac{\sigma_\text{f}}{E} \\ \displaystyle \sigma_\text{f} - E_\text{s} \left(\epsilon - \frac{\sigma_\text{f}}{E}\right) &\epsilon > \epsilon_\text{f} \end{cases} $$

The softening modulus is defined by the strain $\epsilon_0$ at which the stress reaches zero:

$$ E_\text{s} = \frac{\sigma_\text{f}}{\epsilon_0 - \epsilon_\text{f}} $$

Now consider that in the center of the bar there exists a region of length $L_\text{w}$ which is a weak zone with a slightly lower yield strength (or conversely, the remainder of the bar has a slightly higher yield strength). Thus, this element will yield first and then progressively soften.

E = 10.e3 #MPa
sigma_f = 10. #MPa

epsilon_f = lambda E, sf: sf/E
epsilon_0 = 3.*epsilon_f(E,sigma_f)

E_S = lambda E,sf,e0: sf/(e0 - epsilon_f(E,sf))

def stress(eps):
    return E*eps*np.heaviside(epsilon_f(E,sigma_f) - eps,0.5) + (sigma_f - E_S(E,sigma_f,epsilon_0)*(eps - sigma_f/E))*np.heaviside(eps-epsilon_f(E,sigma_f),0.5)

strain = np.linspace(0,epsilon_0,1000)
plt.plot(strain*100.,stress(strain))
plt.tight_layout()
plt.xlabel('$\\epsilon$ / %')
plt.ylabel('$\\sigma$ / MPa');

def dstress_deps(eps):
    return E*np.heaviside(epsilon_f(E,sigma_f) - eps,0.5) - E_S(E,sigma_f,epsilon_0)*np.heaviside(eps-epsilon_f(E,sigma_f),0.5)

L = 100. #mm
A = 1. #mm²

To make our life easier, we prescribe the strain in the weak element, calculate the length-change in that area and its incremental stiffness in order to obtain the force in the bar. As this is equal in all cross sections, we can then compute the total length change.

def calculate_incremental_response(L_w,eps_w):
    F_bar = np.zeros(len(eps_w))
    u_bar = np.zeros(len(eps_w))
    energy_undamaged = np.zeros(len(eps_w))
    energy_damaged = np.zeros(len(eps_w))
    k_w = dstress_deps(eps_w)*A/L_w
    for i in range(1,len(eps_w)):
        #force in bar
        F_bar[i] = np.maximum(F_bar[i-1] + k_w[i-1]*(eps_w[i]-eps_w[i-1])*L_w,0.)
        #strain in undamaged regions
        eps_rest = F_bar[i]/(E*A)
        #displacement at end of bar
        u_bar[i] = eps_rest*(L-L_w) + eps_w[i]*L_w
        #elastic energy stored in undamaged regions:
        energy_undamaged[i] = F_bar[i]*eps_rest*(L-L_w)/2.
        #elastic energy stored in damaged region
        energy_damaged[i] = F_bar[i]*eps_w[i]/2.*L_w
    return u_bar, F_bar, energy_undamaged, energy_damaged

zone_width = [L*0.99,L*0.7,L*0.5,L*0.2,L*0.02]

for i,Lw in enumerate(zone_width):
    u,F,Eu,Ed = calculate_incremental_response(Lw,strain)
    plt.plot(u,F,label='$L_\\mathrm{w} = %.2f$ mm' %Lw)
    
plt.xlabel('$u$ / mm')
plt.ylabel('$F$ / N')
plt.legend()
plt.tight_layout()

zone_width = [L*0.7,L*0.2]
fig,ax = plt.subplots(ncols=4,figsize=(18,6))

for i,Lw in enumerate(zone_width):

Looking at the energy trends, we see that the dissipated energy in the global structure (left) is different. The second image shows that the undamaged parts undergoe elastic unloading, while the third image shows that the elastic energy in the damaged region is not released reversibly. Instead, as seen in the last image, the energy is dissipated there. We also see, that dissipation is zero until damage sets in and that the amount of energy dissipated in the damaged region differs.

A simple fix (handle with care!) is to adjust the dissipation on the material level with the discretization length. In other words, one fixes the dissipation energy of the structure by altering the dissipation at the material level in dependence of the structural size. The dissipated energy on the material level is here:

$$ g_\text{f} = \frac{\sigma_\text{f} \epsilon_\text{f}}{2} + \frac{\sigma_\text{f} (\epsilon_0 - \epsilon_\text{f})}{2} = \frac{\sigma_\text{f} \epsilon_0}{2} $$

and has units of Pa, or J/m³. This energy is dissipated in a volume $V = A L_\text{w}$. The energy release rate is this energy referred to the cross section (damaged area, fracture surface) $A$ and can thus be computed as

$$ G_\text{c} = g_\text{f} L_\text{w} $$

Thus, in order to maintain physical behaviour in the above example, we fix $G_c$ and adjust $\epsilon_0$ depending on $L_\text{w}$:

$$ \epsilon_0 = \frac{2G_\text{c}}{\sigma_\text{f} L_\text{w}} $$

Once again, this is not a recommended procedure but can be a useful quick-fix in certain very controlled circumstances.

Let's say we identified the green curve above as the physical one. We thus fix that value of the dissipated energy per unit area:

G_c = sigma_f * epsilon_0 * (L*0.7)/2.
print('G_c = %.2f N/mm²' %G_c)

G_c = 1.05 N/mm²

zone_width = [L*0.99,L*0.7,L*0.5,L*0.2,L*0.02]

for i,Lw in enumerate(zone_width):
    epsilon_0 = 2.*G_c/(sigma_f * Lw)
    print('Middle element (%.2f mm) strained to %.2f%% to reach structural failure and full dissipation.' %(Lw,epsilon_0*100.))
    strain = np.linspace(0.,epsilon_0,10000)
    u,F,Eu,Ed = calculate_incremental_response(Lw,strain)

The global dissipated energy is now identical in both cases. Elastic unloading stays unaffected (only influenced by relative size of this region), and the dissipated energy in the damaged zone is also identical at the end. However, the strain required differs (dissipation displacement constant).
